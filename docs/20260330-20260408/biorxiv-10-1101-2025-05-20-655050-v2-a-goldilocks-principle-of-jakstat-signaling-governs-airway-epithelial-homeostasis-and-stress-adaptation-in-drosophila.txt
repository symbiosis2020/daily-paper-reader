> Summary

28 

> Airway epithelia must maintain barrier integrity while continuously adapting to

29 

> changing environmental conditions. This requires key signaling pathways in airway

30 

> epithelia to operate within a tightly controlled functional range. Here, we show that

31 

> airway epithelial homeostasis in  Drosophila  depends on balanced JAK/STAT signaling.

32 

> Basal pathway activity is constitutively present in differentiated epithelial cells and is

33 

> necessary for cell survival, epithelial integrity and stress resistance. In accordance

34 

> with this role as a stress responsive regulator in epithelial biology, environmental

35 

> stressors, including hypoxia, cigarette smoke, and cold exposure, induce JAK/STAT

36 

> signaling.  In  contrast,  sustained  pathway  activation  is  associated  with  airway

37 

> remodeling characterized by epithelial thickening, luminal narrowing, and altered

38 

> cellular organization. Transcriptomic analysis reveals that sustained activation is

39 

> associated  with  a coordinated  epithelial  stress  program  integrating  immune

40 

> signaling, proteostasis, and metabolic adaptation. Cross-species comparisons with

41 

> murine and human datasets suggest that key aspects of this response are conserved.

42 

> Pharmacological inhibition demonstrates that remodeling depends on continued

43 

> pathway activity and can be partially reversed  in vivo . Together, our findings support

44 

> a model in which airway epithelial homeostasis depends on maintaining JAK/STAT

45 

> signaling within a defined functional range. This “Goldilocks” principle provides a

46 

> conceptual  framework  for  understanding  how  epithelial  stress  responses  are

47 

> balanced under physiological and pathological conditions.

> Introduction

50 

> Airway epithelia form the first barrier between the organism and the external

51 

> environment and are therefore continuously exposed to environmental stressors,

52 

> including  pathogens,  pollutants,  and  fluctuations  in  oxygen  availability[1,  2].

53 

> Maintaining epithelial integrity under these conditions requires the coordinated

54 

> regulation of signaling pathways that govern epithelial survival, stress responses, and

55 

> tissue architecture[3, 4]. Dysregulation of epithelial signaling can lead to pathological

56 

> remodeling processes that contribute to chronic airway diseases, including asthma

57 

> and chronic obstructive pulmonary disease [5-7]. Understanding how epithelial

58 

> signaling pathways maintain airway homeostasis while enabling adaptive responses

59 

> to environmental stress, therefore, represents a fundamental challenge in lung

60 

> research.

61 

> Among the signaling pathways implicated in epithelial regulation, the JAK/STAT

62 

> signaling pathway plays a central role in epithelial stress responses and tissue

63 

> remodeling across metazoans[8, 9]. In mammalian airways, JAK/STAT signaling is

64 

> activated  by  inflammatory  cytokines  and  has  been  implicated  in  epithelial

65 

> proliferation,  immune  responses,  and  airway  remodeling  during  chronic  lung

66 

> disease[9-11]. However, despite extensive work in mammalian systems, it remains

67 

> unclear how JAK/STAT signaling contributes to airway epithelial homeostasis under

68 

> physiological conditions and how different levels of pathway activity affect epithelial

69 

> structure and function[12-15]

[4] .70 

> The  Drosophila  tracheal system provides a powerful in vivo model to study airway

71 

> epithelial biology. The fly airway epithelium shares key structural and functional

72 

> features  with  mammalian  airway  epithelia  while  offering  unparalleled  genetic

73 

> accessibility to manipulate signaling pathways directly in epithelial cells

[16-21] .74 

> Previous work has established that JAK/STAT signaling is important during tracheal

75 

> morphogenesis[22-24] and that it regulates epithelial responses to infection and

76 

> injury in multiple tissues in  Drosophila [25, 26]. However, the role of this pathway in

77 

> maintaining  airway  epithelial  homeostasis  and  coordinating  responses  to

78 

> environmental stress has not been systematically investigated.

79 

> Here, we use the genetically tractable  Drosophila  airway system to examine how

80 

> JAK/STAT signaling regulates epithelial homeostasis. We show that basal pathway

81 

> activity contributes to epithelial integrity, whereas sustained activation is associated

82 

> with structural remodeling. Furthermore, we demonstrate that environmental stress

83 

> dynamically  modulates  pathway activity and  that  chronic  activation  induces  a

84 

> coordinated epithelial stress program. Based on these findings, we propose that

85 

> airway epithelia require a “Goldilocks” level of JAK/STAT signaling, in which balanced

86 

> pathway activity maintains homeostasis while insufficient or excessive signaling

87 

> disrupts tissue integrity.

> Results

91 

> To investigate how JAK/STAT signaling regulates airway epithelial homeostasis, we

92 

> combined genetic, environmental, and transcriptomic approaches in the  Drosophila

93 

> airway system. We first assessed basal pathway activity and its requirement for

94 

> epithelial integrity. We then examined how environmental stress modulates pathway

95 

> activity, followed by analysis of the structural and molecular consequences of

96 

> sustained pathway activation. Finally, we tested whether pharmacological inhibition

97 

> can reverse the observed remodeling phenotypes.

> JAK/STAT Signaling Is Present throughout the Larval Tracheal System

100 

> To determine whether JAK/STAT signaling is active under physiological conditions,

101 

> we examined pathway activity using a STAT-responsive transcriptional reporter (Fig.

102 

> 1A).  Under  basal  conditions,  the  trachea  of  third-instar  Drosophila  larvae  (L3)

103 

> displayed near-ubiquitous reporter activity across all tracheal compartments (Fig. 1B-

104 

> E).  However,  marked  regional  differences  in  signal  intensity  were  evident.

105 

> Specifically, JAK/STAT signaling was enriched in defined zones of the larval tracheal

106 

> system, including the dorsal trunks, spiracular branches (SB), and Tr2, regions

107 

> previously associated with regenerative capacity 26,27  (Fig. 1D, E). In contrast, tracheal

108 

> regions fated for apoptosis during metamorphosis (e.g., Tr10) exhibited only minimal

109 

> reporter activity (Fig. 1C), highlighting a functional correlation between JAK/STAT

110 

> activity and tissue maintenance.

111 

> In adult flies, reporter analysis revealed sustained pathway activity throughout the

112 

> airway  network  (Fig.  1F),  confirming  a role  for  JAK/STAT  signaling  beyond

113 

> developmental morphogenesis. Transcriptional mapping of  upd2  further localized

114 

> ligand expression to SB and Tr2 tracheoblasts (Fig. S1), consistent with an epithelial-

115 

> intrinsic autocrine regulatory circuit.

116 

> These  observations  indicate  that  JAK/STAT  signaling  is  constitutively  active  in

117 

> differentiated airway epithelial cells, suggesting a potential role in maintaining

118 

> epithelial  homeostasis.  We  next asked  whether  this  basal  pathway  activity  is

119 

> functionally required for airway epithelial integrity.

> Environmental stress activates JAK/STAT signaling in airway epithelial cells

146 

> To determine whether environmental stress regulates epithelial JAK/STAT signaling,

147 

> we exposed animals to several physiologically relevant stress conditions. To evaluate

148 

> the responsiveness of airway epithelial cells to environmental insults, we employed a

149 

> GFP-based reporter driven by STAT92E activation in  Drosophila . Exposure to airborne

150 

> stressors, including cold air, hypoxia, and cigarette smoke extract (CSE), triggered

151 

> robust and reproducible pathway activation in larval tracheae (Fig. 3A, B) . Similar

152 

> results were observed in adult flies (Fig. 3C, D). Quantitative imaging confirmed a

153

154

155

156 

> much smaller effects (Fig. S3). To test whether a simple intra-organ signaling system

157

158

159

160 

> pathway (Fig. 3E, F), which implies that this system is more complex, including other

161

162

215 

> Transcriptomic analysis of airway epithelial cells with sustained JAK/STAT activation

216

217 

> GEO database (GSE212132). Upregulated genes included canonical pathway targets as

218 

> well as genes associated with immune responses, stress pathways, and metabolism.

227

> Discussion

304 

> Basal Homeostasis and Epithelial Viability

305 

> Leveraging the genetically streamlined architecture of  Drosophila melanogaster , this

306 

> study uncovers the bifunctional role of the JAK/STAT cascade in airway epithelia.

307 

> Because the system relies on a single receptor (Domeless), kinase (Hopscotch), and

308 

> transcription factor (STAT92E) [34], we circumvented vertebrate redundancy [35] to

309 

> dissect pathway dynamics at high resolution. Our data indicate that JAK/STAT

310 

> signaling operates within a narrow functional range in airway epithelial cells. Basal

311 

> pathway activity is required to maintain epithelial integrity and organismal stress

312 

> resistance, whereas sustained hyperactivation induces structural remodeling and

313 

> disrupts epithelial organization. Together, these findings support a “Goldilocks”

314 

> model in which balanced JAK/STAT signaling preserves airway homeostasis, while

315 

> both insufficient and excessive activity impairs epithelial function.

316 

> This requirement for basal activity perfectly aligns with recent mammalian findings

317 

> demonstrating  that tonic JAK/STAT  signaling is critical for maintaining cellular

318 

> homeostasis and identity across immune and epithelial compartments [36, 37].

319 

> Furthermore, our observation that early pathway disruption causes catastrophic

320 

> architectural defects during tracheal tube formation echoes vertebrate studies

321 

> establishing STAT3 as a fundamental requirement for lung organogenesis and

322 

> epithelial lineage maintenance [12, 38].

327 

> cigarette smoke extract (CSE) induced robust activation of JAK/STAT signaling in

328 

> airway epithelial cells. This response was associated with increased expression of

329 

> Upd ligands, suggesting that epithelial cells actively participate in amplifying stress

> A Goldilocks Principle of JAK/STAT Signaling in Airway Epithelia

340 

> Our findings support a “Goldilocks” principle  of JAK/STAT signaling  in airway

341 

> epithelia, in which both insufficient and excessive pathway activity compromise

342 

> tissue integrity. Basal signaling appears to support epithelial survival and stress

343 

> resistance, whereas chronic hyperactivation is associated with epithelial remodeling

344 

> and functional disruption.

345 

> This conceptual framework provides a unifying explanation for the dual roles of

346 

> JAK/STAT signaling observed in both physiological and pathological contexts. While

347 

> acute activation likely supports adaptive responses, sustained signaling may shift the

348 

> system toward a maladaptive state. This echoes the delicate balance of the JAK/STAT

349 

> pathway in airway epithelia [12]. If the pathway fails to activate, the organism is

350 

> heavily sensitized to chronic stress. Conversely, as seen in chronic lung conditions like

351 

> asthma and COPD where the JAK/STAT pathway remains persistently hyperactive [10,

352 

> 40], the protective stress response transforms into pathology.

353 

354 

> Stress-Induced Transcriptional Reprogramming

355 

> Transcriptomic profiling of airway epithelial cells with sustained JAK/STAT activation

356 

> revealed a coordinated transcriptional response characterized by upregulation of

357 

> innate immune effectors, stress response pathways, and metabolic regulators. These

358 

> findings suggest that chronic pathway activation induces a broad epithelial stress

359 

> program rather than a purely immune-specific response.

360 

> Comparison with previously described gene sets, including immune and stress-

361 

> related signatures, revealed significant overlap, indicating that JAK/STAT activation

362 

> engages conserved regulatory modules [30, 31]. The transcriptional reprogramming

363 

> induced by sustained JAK/STAT activation seems to fundamentally alter the cellular

364 

> identity, shifting it into a systemic, stress-adapted state that closely mimics the

365 

> inflammatory landscapes seen in idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis and acute lung injury

366 

> [41, 42]. However, these overlaps are based on orthology mapping and should be

367 

> interpreted cautiously, as species-specific differences in gene regulation are not fully

368 

> captured.

> Our  data  support  a model  in  which  sustained JAK/STAT  activation  induces  a

372 

> coordinated stress response that increases secretory demand and proteostatic load

373

381

383 

> processes,  the  data  support  a mechanistic  link  between  sustained  pathway

> activation, cellular stress responses, and structural remodeling.

> Conservation Across Species and Disease Contexts

387 

> Cross-species comparisons revealed overlap between the transcriptional signatures

388 

> induced by JAK/STAT activation in  Drosophila  and those observed in murine asthma

389 

> models and cytokine-stimulated human airway epithelia. These shared features

390 

> include activation of immune signaling pathways, stress responses, and alterations in

391 

> epithelial transport and metabolism.

392 

> While these comparisons rely on computational mapping and do not imply direct

393 

> equivalence, they suggest that core aspects of epithelial stress responses may be

394 

> conserved across species. This supports the use of the  Drosophila  airway system as a

395 

> model to investigate general principles of epithelial remodeling.

396 

397 

> Pharmacological Modulation and Translational Implications

398 

> Pharmacological  inhibition  experiments  demonstrated  that  airway  remodeling

399 

> induced by sustained JAK/STAT activation can be partially reversed in vivo. Notably,

400 

> inhibitors with higher specificity toward JAK1-like activity showed greater efficacy in

401 

> reducing epithelial thickening. These results precisely mirror the specific kinase

402 

> binding profiles established in vertebrate models [44-46]. This functional divergence

403 

> suggests that the hyperactive  Drosophila  Hop.CA kinase shares a profound structural

404 

> or functional homology with vertebrate JAK1, establishing this in vivo model as a

405 

> highly specific screening platform for next-generation, JAK1-targeted respiratory

406 

> therapeutics [16, 17, 47]. Although inhibition of the JAK/STAT signaling pathway is

407 

> currently being actively explored as a therapeutic strategy, particularly for chronic

408 

> forms of asthma that are refractory to conventional treatments [48], it may interfere

409 

> with the regenerative capacity of the airway epithelium [13]. Thus, therapeutic

410 

> strategies will likely require careful modulation rather than complete inhibition.

> Conclusion

413 

> In summary, our findings define a narrow functional window for JAK/STAT signaling

414 

> in airway epithelia, in which balanced pathway activity is required to maintain

415 

> epithelial integrity and stress resilience. Deviations from this balance—either through

416 

> insufficient or excessive signaling—lead to epithelial dysfunction and structural

417 

> remodeling.  This  “Goldilocks”  principle  provides  a conceptual  framework  for

418 

> understanding  how  epithelial  tissues  integrate  environmental  signals  while

419 

> preserving homeostasis and offers insights into conserved mechanisms underlying

420 

> airway disease.
